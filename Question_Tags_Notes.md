# Question Tags — Complete Revision Notes
*Error-spotting / fill-in-the-blank focus for competitive exams*

---

## 1. What Is a Question Tag & Why We Use It

**Name origin:** "Tag" means to attach/add something small (like a price tag on clothes). A question tag is a **small tag attached at the end of a statement**.

**Important — Question tags are NOT used to ask a new question.** Despite the name, they are used for:
- **Confirmation** (पुष्टि करना)
- **Seeking agreement / certainty** about something we've already said

We use a tag to make the listener confirm what we just said, not to genuinely ask something new.

**Examples:**
1. *You should work hard, shouldn't you?* (confirming, not asking something new)
2. *He works hard, doesn't he?* (seeking agreement)
3. *You want selections, don't you?* (confirming the listener's goal)
4. *He will come tomorrow, won't he?* (confirming a statement about the future)

---

## 2. Two Things Needed to Build a Question Tag

To form a question tag, we need:
1. A **Verb** (to build the auxiliary structure)
2. A **Pronoun** (never a noun — the subject in the tag is always replaced by its pronoun)

> **In order to make a question tag, we don't need a noun, we need a pronoun.**

**Examples:**
1. *Ram is a good boy, isn't **he**?* (noun "Ram" → pronoun "he" in the tag)
2. *The girls are dancing, aren't **they**?* (noun "girls" → "they")
3. *Rahul and Mohan play well, don't **they**?* (two nouns → plural pronoun "they")
4. *The dog is barking, isn't **it**?* (noun "dog" → "it", non-living/animal treated as "it")

---

## 3. The Golden/Basic Rule

> **If the sentence is Positive, the tag is Negative.**
> **If the sentence is Negative, the tag is Positive.**

This is the foundation on which everything else is built.

**Examples:**
1. *She sings well, doesn't she?* (positive sentence → negative tag)
2. *He does not like tea, does he?* (negative sentence → positive tag)
3. *They are playing football, aren't they?* (positive → negative)
4. *You never lie, do you?* (negative sense → positive tag)

---

## 4. Choosing the Verb for the Tag: Auxiliary vs Do/Does/Did

### Rule A — If the sentence already has an Auxiliary (helping) Verb + Main Verb
Use the **auxiliary verb** to build the tag (in its negative or positive short form as required).

**Examples:**
1. *The students were doing their work, weren't they?* (auxiliary "were" used for tag)
2. *The teacher is teaching, isn't she?* (auxiliary "is")
3. *She has written the letter, hasn't she?* (auxiliary "has")
4. *They will come tomorrow, won't they?* (auxiliary "will")

### Rule B — If the sentence has NO auxiliary verb (only a Main Verb)
It's **not necessary that every sentence has an auxiliary verb**. When there's no auxiliary, build the tag using **Do / Does / Did**, chosen based on tense and number:

| Situation | Tag verb |
|---|---|
| Present tense, plural subject | **don't/do** |
| Present tense, singular subject | **doesn't/does** |
| Past tense (any subject, singular or plural) | **didn't/did** |

**Examples:**
1. *Children like to eat sweets, don't they?* (present, plural "children" → don't)
2. *The Earth revolves around the Sun, doesn't it?* (present, singular "Earth" → doesn't)
3. *The student did the work, didn't he?* (past tense → didn't)
4. *She writes a letter every day, doesn't she?* (present, singular → doesn't)

## 5. Words That Make a Sentence Negative (Other Than No/Not/Never)

Normally we assume a sentence is negative only when we see **no, not, never**. But several other words also make a sentence **negative in meaning**, even though they don't look negative at first glance. When these words appear, the **tag becomes positive** (because the sentence is secretly negative).

**Negative words to remember:**
- hardly, scarcely, barely, rarely, seldom (= "almost never / very rarely" — near-negative adverbs)
- nobody, nothing, none, no one
- little, few *(without "a" — see next section)*

**Examples:**
1. *He hardly comes on time, does he?* ("hardly" = negative → positive tag)
2. *It hardly rains here, does it?* ("hardly" → positive tag)
3. *That dog seldom bites, does it?* ("seldom" = negative → positive tag)
4. *She scarcely speaks to anyone, does she?* ("scarcely" = negative → positive tag)

## 6. Little/Few (Negative) vs A Little/A Few (Positive)

This is a **very important trap**.

- **Little** (uncountable) and **Few** (countable) — **WITHOUT the article "a"** — are **negative** words (they mean "almost none / hardly any").
- **A little** and **A few** — **WITH the article "a"** — are **positive** words (they mean "at least some, a small amount exists").

> Whenever an article ("a") is added, the word becomes positive.

**Examples:**
1. *He has little knowledge of the subject, does he?* ("little" without "a" = negative → positive tag)
2. *There is little milk left in the bottle, is there?* (negative → positive tag)
3. *He has a little knowledge of the subject, doesn't he?* ("a little" = positive → negative tag)
4. *There is a little money left, isn't there?* ("a little" = positive → negative tag)

*(Same logic applies to "few" vs "a few" for countable nouns: "Few students passed, did they?" [negative] vs "A few students passed, didn't they?" [positive].)*

## 7. Causative "Had/Have/Has" (acts as Main Verb, not Auxiliary)

"Had / have / has" can play **two different roles**:
1. **Auxiliary verb** — followed directly by V3 (Past/Present Perfect): *I had cleaned the house.*
2. **Causative / main verb** ("get something done through someone else") — here an **object/noun comes in between** "had/have/has" and V3: *I had the house cleaned* (meaning: I got the house cleaned by someone else).

**How to identify:**
- If V3 comes **immediately** after had/have/has → it's an **auxiliary** → use had/have/has itself to build the tag.
- If a **noun/object appears between** had/have/has and V3 → it's a **causative (main) verb**, NOT an auxiliary → use **did/does/do** to build the tag instead.

**Examples:**
1. *I had cleaned the house, hadn't I?* (had = auxiliary, V3 immediately after → use "had" in tag)
2. *I had the house cleaned, didn't I?* (had = causative main verb, object "the house" comes in between → use "did" in tag)
3. *She has written the report, hasn't she?* (has = auxiliary → use "has")
4. *She has her car washed every week, doesn't she?* (has = causative, object "her car" in between → use "does")

## 8. Collective Nouns — Singular or Plural Depending on Context

Certain nouns (team, jury, panel, board, committee, council, mob, government, parliament, class, family) can be **either singular or plural**, depending on whether the group is acting/thinking **as one unit** or **as separate individuals**.

- **One unit / unanimous** → treat as **singular** (tag uses "it")
- **Acting/thinking separately / divided opinion** → treat as **plural** (tag uses "they")

**Examples:**
1. *The team won the match unitedly, didn't it?* (acting as one unit → singular "it")
2. *The team members wore different uniforms, didn't they?* (acting separately → plural "they")
3. *The jury gave a unanimous opinion, didn't it?* (all agree, single opinion → singular)
4. *The jury gave a divided opinion, didn't they?* (opinions differ among members → plural)

## 9. Apostrophe Forms — Deciding What They Stand For

### 'S — can mean IS, HAS, or WAS (decide from context)
- Followed by **V-ing** → almost always **is** (or **was**, if a past time marker like "yesterday" is present in the sentence).
- Followed by **V3** → usually **has** (Present Perfect, active voice). If a clear past-time word is present, it may actually need **was** instead (check the sentence carefully — don't assume 's always means "is" or "has").

**Examples:**
1. *It's raining heavily today, isn't it?* ('s + V-ing, present context → "is")
2. *He's finished his homework, hasn't he?* ('s + V3, active voice → "has")
3. *She was busy yesterday, wasn't she?* (past-time word "yesterday" → full form "was", not treated as 's = is)
4. *The work's being done properly, isn't it?* ('s + being + V3 = passive continuous → "is")

### 'D — can mean WOULD or HAD (decide from context)
- Followed by **V1 (base form)** → **would**
- Followed by **V3** → **had** (Past Perfect) — UNLESS it's a **causative** structure (object between 'd and V3), in which case use **did** for the tag instead (see Section 7).

**Examples:**
1. *I'd like to have tea rather than coffee, wouldn't I?* ('d + V1 "like" → would)
2. *I'd completed the work before he came, hadn't I?* ('d + V3 "completed" → had)
3. *He'd go home early if he were tired, wouldn't he?* ('d + V1 "go" → would)
4. *She'd her hair cut yesterday, didn't she?* ('d + object "her hair" + V3 "cut" = causative → did, not had)

## 10. Imperative Sentences (Orders / Requests / Commands)

Imperative sentences (commands, requests, advice) follow a **special pattern**, different from the usual "positive → negative tag" rule.

- **Negative imperative** (e.g., *Don't...*) → tag is **always positive**: **"will you"** (never "won't you").
- **Positive imperative** (an order/request in positive form) → **both** "will you" and "won't you" are grammatically acceptable (either can be correct — exams will not test both as competing correct options in the same question).
- If the command is spoken with **irritation/anger**, "**can you**" (or "can't you") may replace "will you"/"won't you".

**Examples:**
1. *Don't shout in the class, will you?* (negative imperative → always "will you")
2. *Close the door, will you?* (positive imperative → "will you" is fine)
3. *Close the door, won't you?* (positive imperative → "won't you" is also fine)
4. *Shut your mouth, can't you?* (spoken in irritation/anger → "can't you")

## 11. "Let's" — Always Takes "Shall We"

When a sentence begins with **"Let's" (Let us)**, the tag is **always "shall we?"**, regardless of whether the sentence looks positive or negative. This is a fixed/constant exception to the usual positive-negative flip rule.

**Examples:**
1. *Let's go for a walk, shall we?*
2. *Let's not waste any more time, shall we?*
3. *Let's start the class, shall we?*
4. *Let's finish this chapter today, shall we?*

---

## 12. "One" as Subject — Adjective or Pronoun

"One" can function as an **adjective** (a determiner, e.g., *one boy*) or as a **pronoun** (standing in place of a noun).

- When "**one**" is a pronoun meaning **"each person / everyone" (a general/universal statement)** → the tag also uses "**one**" (not "he" or "they").
- When "**one**" refers to **a single specific person from a group** ("one of the students") → the tag uses "**he**" (singular, gender defaults to "he" when unclear), never "they" (since "one" is always singular).

**Examples:**
1. *One should keep one's promise, shouldn't one?* ("one" = everyone, general truth → tag repeats "one")
2. *Everyone should respect one's elders, shouldn't one?* (general truth → "one")
3. *One of the boys did his work, didn't he?* ("one" = a specific single person → "he")
4. *One of the students has finished the test, hasn't he?* (specific single person from a group → "he")

## 13. Indefinite Pronouns — People vs Things

| Type | Words | Tag Pronoun |
|---|---|---|
| **People** | anybody/anyone, everybody/everyone, somebody/someone, nobody/no one | **they** (plural, even though the word itself is grammatically singular) |
| **Things** | anything, everything, something, nothing | **it** (singular) |

Both categories are singular in themselves (so a singular verb is often used in the main clause), but **only the "people" group takes a plural pronoun ("they") in the tag** — the "things" group keeps a singular pronoun ("it").

Also remember: **nobody** and **nothing** are inherently **negative** → the tag must be **positive**.

**Examples:**
1. *Everybody was present in the class, weren't they?* (people → "they")
2. *Nobody was present, were they?* (people + negative word "nobody" → positive tag, pronoun "they")
3. *Everything is fine, isn't it?* (thing → "it")
4. *Nothing is impossible, is it?* (thing + negative word "nothing" → positive tag, pronoun "it")

## 14. "All of / Some of + Pronoun" Constructions

When the subject is "**all of / some of + you/them/us**", the tag pronoun **matches the pronoun already used in the sentence** (you → you, them → they, us → we) — since these are already plural in sense.

**Examples:**
1. *All of you were present, weren't you?*
2. *All of them completed the task, didn't they?*
3. *Some of us are going to the market, aren't we?*
4. *All of us were happy, weren't we?*

---

## 15. This/That/These/Those as Subject vs "There is/are"

- **This / That** as the subject of the sentence → tag uses **"it"**.
- **These / Those** as the subject of the sentence → tag uses **"they"**.
- **BUT** — if "this/that" is only **introducing** another subject (e.g., *This is my friend, Ram*), the tag should match the **real subject being introduced** (Ram → he), not "it".
- **"There is / There are"** (existential sentences) → **keep "there" itself** in the tag — do NOT replace it with "it"/"they".

**Examples:**
1. *This is a nice book, isn't it?* ("this" is the actual subject → "it")
2. *These are your books, aren't they?* ("these" is the actual subject → "they")
3. *This is my friend, Ram, isn't he?* ("this" only introduces "Ram", the real subject → "he", not "it")
4. *There is a book on the table, isn't there?* (existential "there" sentence → keep "there" in the tag)

---

## 16. Complex Sentences — Tag Based on the Main/Content Clause

When a sentence has **two clauses** — an opinion/reporting clause (*I think, I believe, I suppose, I hope, I am afraid, it seems, I guess*) plus a **main content clause** — the tag is built from the **main content clause**, NOT from the opinion clause. This is because the opinion clause ("I think...") is not really being confirmed — the content it introduces is.

**Examples:**
1. *I think he is right, isn't he?* (main content = "he is right" → tag on that, not "don't I")
2. *I believe they are the best, aren't they?* (main content = "they are the best")
3. *I hope he does the work on time, doesn't he?* (main content = "he does the work on time")
4. *I suppose India will win the match, won't it?* (main content = "India will win the match")
