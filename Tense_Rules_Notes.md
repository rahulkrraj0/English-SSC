# English Tense — Complete Notes (Error-Spotting Focus)
*Based on lecture: Tense from perspective of error-spotting, not translation*

---

## 1. Tense — Basic Concept

**Definition (collective):** Tense tells **about the time of an action as well as the state of an action.**
- **Time** → Past / Present / Future (3 broad types)
- **State** → whether the action is complete or incomplete (this is why each of the 3 is further split into 4 forms)

Since "Tense" itself means time, and Verb already covers action, Tense is **not** a separate Part of Speech — it's expressed *through* the verb.

### Why each tense has 4 sub-types
Simple Past/Present/Future alone only tell **time**. The 4 sub-forms of each tell **state** (complete/incomplete):

| Form | Name (alt) | State it shows |
|---|---|---|
| Simple | Indefinite | Neither complete nor incomplete is clear |
| Continuous | Imperfect | Incomplete, action still going on |
| Perfect | Perfect | Action complete |
| Perfect Continuous | — | Started in past, still continuing (started at a *fixed* point) |

- **Indefinite** = "अनिश्चित" — cyclic/habitual action, no clear start/end (e.g. *He goes to school*)
- **Continuous/Imperfect** = action going on now, at least incomplete is known (e.g. *He is going to school*)
- **Perfect** = action is complete (e.g. *He has gone to school*)
- **Perfect Continuous** = complete + continuous both — action started at a **fixed time** and is still going on (e.g. *He has been reading a book since 2 pm* — Perfect Continuous tells *since when*; plain Perfect does not make the start time clear)

## 2. Two Biggest Areas of Student Confusion
1. Simple Past vs Past Perfect — kab konsa lagana hai
2. Present Perfect vs Past Perfect — kab konsa lagana hai
3. Tense is "an game of sense" — dono parts of a sentence same tense mein hona zaroori nahi

### 🔑 Master Rule (very important)
- Jab **TIME** par zor diya ja raha ho → **Simple (Indefinite)** tense
- Jab **ACTION** par zor diya ja raha ho (कि काम हो चुका है) → **Perfect / Perfect Continuous**

Example:
- *He went to school* ✅ *He has gone to school* ✅ (both correct, no time word)
- *He went to school **yesterday*** ✅ (correct — time stressed) | *He has gone to school yesterday* ❌ (wrong — perfect + specific past time word don't mix)
- BUT: *He **had** left for the airport yesterday before I reached home* ✅ — here yesterday isn't stressing time alone, it's connecting **two actions** (leaving + reaching), so Past Perfect is fine.

**Takeaway:** "Yesterday" sentence is NOT always Simple Past — check the *sense* (single time-emphasis vs two linked actions).

## Chapter 2: Simple Present (Present Indefinite)

**Use for:**
- Habitual/routine actions (daily habits)
- Universal truths (*The sun rises in the east*)
- General statements, proverbs (*Honesty is the best policy*)
- Conditional sentences (if-clause part)

### Extra uses (exam traps):
1. **Near/Immediate Future** (fixed timetable/formal events):
   - *The PM leaves for Mumbai next month* ✅ (formal, official tour)
   - *The match starts at 8 pm* ✅ (fixed schedule)
2. **Past→Present shift for headlines** (to highlight/dramatize):
   - *India wins a glorious victory* (match was played yesterday, but headline uses present)
   - Only applies to **headlines**, not to the body/detail of the news (body stays in past)

### Simple Present vs Present Continuous (both can be used, watch nuance)
- Formal fixed event (official tour, match start) → **both** Simple Present and Present Continuous OK
  - *The PM leaves for Mumbai next month* / *The PM is leaving for Mumbai next month* — both correct
- **Informal** personal event (e.g., "my birthday party is next week") → only **Present Continuous**, never Simple Present
  - *I am having a birthday party next week* ✅ | *I have a birthday party next week* ❌ (too casual for simple present's "official announcement" sense)

### Permanent vs Temporary Action (Simple Present vs Present Continuous)
- *He teaches English* = permanent job/habitual (Simple Present)
- *He is teaching English* = temporary action today, may not even be his subject (Present Continuous)

### Negative-sense repeated action
- Positive habitual action → **always Simple Present** (never Continuous)
  - *He always helps me* ✅ | *He is always helping me* ❌
- Negative habitual/repeated action (mistake, complaint) → **both** Simple Present and Continuous OK
  - *He always makes the same mistake* ✅ | *He is always making the same mistake* ✅ (continuous adds emphasis/intensity of repeated fault)

### Conditional Sentences — Golden Rule
- A conditional sentence never has **two future (will)** forms.
- Structure: **If + Simple Present, ... Simple Future**
  - *If he comes, I will go* ✅ (not *If he will come, I will go*)
- Words that can create a conditional sense (besides "if"): *when, whenever, after, until, unless, provided (that), in case*
- **Both parts can be Simple Present** when the sentence expresses a fact/universal truth (not just a personal condition):
  - *If you heat water, it boils at 100°C* ✅ (fact, not a real future condition)

### If ≠ always Condition
- Sometimes "if" expresses **doubt/uncertainty**, not a condition → **will** IS allowed with it:
  - *I don't know if he will come* ✅ (doubt, not condition)
- Only avoid "will" after "if" when "if" is genuinely conditional.

### Imaginary / Hypothetical sentences (with if)
- These are NOT covered by the "no two futures" rule the same way — they use **Simple Past** (V2) in the if-clause, regardless of subject:
  - *If I were a bird, I would fly*
  - Note: with imaginary "if", singular subjects also take **"were"** (plural form of V2), not "was":
    - *If he were a doctor, he would help the poor* ✅ (not "was")
  - Same idea applies to *I wish...*, and to *as if / as though* sentences (imaginary comparison) — these take Simple Past too.

## Chapter 3: Present Continuous

**Formula:** is/am/are + V-ing
**Use:** Action happening right now / Near future (train about to enter platform, etc.)

### Words/Verbs whose "-ing" form is WRONG (Stative Verbs)
Stative verbs show a **state**, not an ongoing action — they normally have **no continuous form**.

Key stative verbs and their exceptions:

| Verb | -ing usually WRONG when... | -ing OK when meaning changes to... |
|---|---|---|
| **have** | showing possession/ownership (*He is having two books* ❌ → *He has two books*) | eating (*having breakfast* ✅), suffering (*having fever* ✅), attending (*having a meeting* ✅), enjoying (*having a nice time* ✅) |
| **know** | never takes -ing (statutory stative — *I am knowing him* ❌ always) | "Knowing" as a gerund/noun is fine: *Knowing the truth, I came to a decision* ✅ |
| **see** | meaning "to look/observe" is fine as -ing when active (*I am watching the movie*); but "see" itself (perceive) doesn't take -ing: *I am seeing the movie* ❌ → use **watching** | "see" = meet (*I am seeing her today* ✅), or as subject/gerund: *Seeing is believing* ✅ |
| **hear** | meaning "to listen" — no -ing (*hearing* ❌ for listening) → use **listen/listening** | meaning "a legal hearing" (sunwai) — *The judge is hearing the case* ✅ |
| **belong** | showing possession (*This belongs to me*) — no -ing | — |
| **hope** | stative sense (*I am hoping for the best* ❌) — actually "hoping" IS allowed as a set/greeting expression (*Hoping for the best*, in cards) — check context | — |
| **believe** | stative — no -ing (*Some people are believing...* ❌) | when it's a subject complement/noun form: OK |
| **like/love** | stative — no -ing (*I am loving it* ❌ → *I love it*) | — |
| **understand** | stative — no -ing (*He is not understanding* ❌ → *He does not understand*) | — |
| **appear** | meaning "seem/perceive" — no -ing (*It is appearing to me* ❌ → *It appears to me*) | meaning "take part / rise" (participate in exam, sunrise) — -ing is fine: *He is appearing in the exam*, *Stars are appearing/rising* |

**General rule:** Verb of Action → gets -ing. Verb of State (stative) → no -ing, UNLESS the same word is being used with a different (action) meaning in that sentence.

## Chapter 4: Present Perfect

**Formula:** has/have + V3
**Function:** Connects Past to Present — action started in the past, effect/link continues till now ("तब से लेकर अब तक")

### Key signal words (always take Present Perfect):
- already, just, yet, so far, up till now
- since / for (when connecting past point to present)

### Present Perfect vs Simple Past — the core distinction
- **Time emphasized** → Simple Past (*He went yesterday*)
- **Action/result emphasized, no specific time given** → Present Perfect (*He has gone*)
- If a time word (yesterday, an hour ago) appears in the sentence and it emphasizes *time* → Simple Past, not Present Perfect

### "Time" (baar/frequency) vs "Time" (samay)
- "**time**" as in **frequency** (three *times*, two *times*) does NOT trigger Simple Past — it's compatible with **Present Perfect**:
  - *I have seen this movie three times* ✅ (frequency, not a specific time point)
- "time" as a specific point (samay) → triggers Simple Past:
  - *He left the airport yesterday* (specific time point) → Simple Past

### Negative sentences with "not yet completed" sense
- *He has not completed the work (yet)* → Present Perfect (default for such negative "not yet" sentences)

### Already / Recently / Lately / Of late
- All these normally trigger **Present Perfect**
- Be careful with subject-verb number: *There **has** been no news of recovery* (news = singular, not "have")

### "Today / This week" type sentences
- If the day/week/period is **still ongoing**, Present Perfect gets priority over Simple Past when both fit:
  - *I have seen her today* (preferred over *I saw her today*, because "today" hasn't ended)
  - *I have worked this week* (preferred, because "this week" is still running)

## Chapter 5: Present Perfect Continuous

**Formula:** has/have been + V-ing
**Function:** Also connects past→present, but the action is **still ongoing** (unlike plain Present Perfect where it may have ended)

- *He has been working for two hours* (still working, started 2 hrs ago)
- vs *He has worked for two hours* (work is done/finished)

### Key trap: "know" has no continuous form even here
- *I have been knowing him since January* ❌ → *I have known him since January* ✅ (know = stative, never -ing, even in perfect continuous)

### When BOTH Present Perfect and Present Perfect Continuous work
- If the underlying number/count is not fixed (ongoing habitual pattern): both fine
  - *He has been living in this town for 2 years* / *He has lived in this town for 2 years* — both OK
  - *The player has been scoring century after century since 2016* ✅ (ongoing)
- If a **fixed/completed count** is mentioned: only Present Perfect (simple) works, not continuous
  - *The player has scored 10 centuries since 2016* ✅ (fixed number → action done) — NOT "has been scoring 10 centuries"

## Chapter 6: Simple Past

**Use:** Completed past action, no connection to present. Common time markers (always Simple Past, never Present Perfect):
- yesterday (usually), ago, last week/night/month, formerly, the other day

### "Yesterday" — not always Simple Past!
- If the sentence links two actions (one before another), "yesterday" sentence can take **Past Perfect**:
  - *He had left for the airport yesterday before I reached home* ✅

### Fixed exam phrases that ALWAYS take Simple Past (V2), even though they look like they could be different tenses:
- **It's time (that)...**, **It's high time...**, **It's about time...** → followed by Simple Past (V2)
  - *It's time he **went** to school* ✅ (not "goes"/"has gone")
  - *It's high time he **did** the work* ✅
  - Exception: if **infinitive** form follows directly ("It's time to go") — no tense change needed, infinitive stays infinitive.

### Discovery/historical-event sentences — NOT Past Perfect
- One-time historic/specific-year events (Newton's discovery, Gandhi starting a movement) take **Simple Past**, not Past Perfect, **unless** another action is being linked with before/after:
  - *Newton discovered the law of gravitation* ✅ (Simple Past)
  - *Gandhiji **had** started the Non-Cooperation Movement before the Civil Disobedience Movement* ✅ (Past Perfect — because linked to another past event)

## Chapter 7: Past Perfect (Relative Tense)

**Formula:** had + V3
**Why "relative":** Past Perfect always links **two actions** — one may be explicitly mentioned, or just understood (e.g., "The train had left" — understood: before I reached).

### Core Rule (Golden Rule #1)
When two actions occur in the past:
- Action that happened **first** → **Past Perfect** (had + V3)
- Action that happened **later/second** → **Simple Past** (V2)

*Doctor ke aane se pehle patient mar gaya:*
- Patient died first → **had died**
- Doctor came after → **came** (Simple Past)
(Note: sequence depends on sentence meaning — don't blindly assume "patient always dies before doctor arrives"; read context, e.g. "after the doctor came" reverses the order.)

### Identifying which action came first (without "before/after")
Use logical sense of the sentence:
- *On reaching the campus, he was disappointed (as the bus had left)* — bus leaving happened first → had left

### Simultaneous actions — do NOT use Past Perfect
If two actions happened **at the same time** (not one after another), use **Simple Past** for both:
- *As soon as he stepped onto the stage, it collapsed* → both Simple Past (*stepped*, *collapsed*), NOT "had stepped"

### If reference to an earlier period + "at that time" (comparison up to a point) → Past Perfect
- *That was the worst film I **had seen*** ✅ (comparing against all films seen up to that point — two time-periods linked)

## Chapter 8: Golden Rule Structures (Conditional / Wish type — Past)

### 1. Two "will" never in one sentence (already covered) → in past form:
- **If + Simple Past, ... would + V1**
  - *If he came, I would go*
- Both parts can also be Simple Past with no would (simple past narration): *If he came, I went* (reporting habitual past)

### 2. Two "would have" never in one sentence
- **If + had + V3, ... would have + V3**
  - *If he had come, I would have gone*
- If-clause = Past Perfect (had + V3); main clause = would have/should have/could have/might have + V3
- Modal choice depends on mood: could (ability), might (possibility), should (advice/obligation), would (willingness)

### Meaning difference: Simple Past+would vs Past Perfect+would have
- *He said he would come* → Indefinite/uncertain — unclear whether he came or not
- *He said he would have come* → Perfect — implies action is over: he was supposed to come at that occasion but **didn't** (opportunity has passed)

### Inversion (dropping "if")
- Sentences can become conditional by inverting subject-verb order instead of using "if":
  - *Had he come, I would have gone* = *If he had come, I would have gone* (both correct)
  - *Should he come, I will go* = *If he comes, I will go*
- **Rule:** If you use "if", don't also invert. Use either "if" OR inversion, not both.

### Two "had" in a sentence — allowed! Two "have"/"has" — NOT allowed
- **had** can act as an **auxiliary** OR as a **main verb** (meaning "to take/eat", e.g. *had lunch*) — so a sentence CAN have two "had"s:
  - *He **had had** lunch* ✅ (1st had = auxiliary/Past Perfect marker, 2nd had = main verb "ate")
- **have/has** can also act as auxiliary or main verb, BUT two have's/has's in one sentence is WRONG, because:
  - If "have/has" is auxiliary, it must be followed by V3 (past participle)
  - The V3 (past participle) of "have" is "**had**", not "have/has"
  - So: *I have had lunch* ✅ (NOT "I have have lunch" ❌)
  - Similarly: *He has had lunch* ✅ (NOT "He has has lunch" ❌)

**Quick identification:**
- V1 → has (singular)/have (plural) in Present
- V2 → had (Simple Past)
- V3 → had (Past Perfect marker) — same word as V2 for "have"
- V4 → having (continuous)

## Chapter 9: Past Perfect Continuous

**Formula:** had been + V-ing
**Function:** Same idea as Present Perfect Continuous, just shifted to past — action started at a fixed past point and was **still continuing** at another past point (not up to now).

- *He had been living in this town for 2 years (before I joined)* — action was ongoing in the past, not up to present
- Contrast: if the sentence's time reference is "up to now" (present) → use **has been** (Present Perfect Continuous); if it's "up to a past point" (e.g., before another past event) → use **had been**

**Trap:** Don't assume "has been" just because the surface sentence looks present — read the *whole* sentence (e.g., "...before I joined" signals the sentence is anchored in the past, so it must be **had been**, not has been).

## Chapter 10: Future Tense

### Simple Future (Future Indefinite)
**Formula:** will/shall + V1
- Basic future actions: *I will do the work*
- Simple Present can also express near/fixed future (timetable sense) — already covered above.

### Future Continuous
**Formula:** will be + V-ing
- Action that will be ongoing at a future point: *He will be watching the movie at that time*

### Future Perfect
**Formula:** will have + V3
- Action that will be **completed** by a specific future point
- Trigger word: **"by"** (by next year, by the end of vacation, by this time)
  - *By next year, the students **will have qualified*** the exam ✅ (not simple future "will qualify" — because "by" signals completion by that point)
  - *He will have read the book by the end of vacation* ✅

### Future Perfect Continuous
**Formula:** will have been + V-ing
- Action that will have been going on continuously up to a future point.

## Quick Revision Table

| Tense | Formula | Core Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Simple Present | V1/V1+s | habits, universal truth, if-clause, fixed near future |
| Present Continuous | is/am/are + V-ing | happening now, near future (informal) |
| Present Perfect | has/have + V3 | already/just/yet/so far/since/for, action emphasis |
| Present Perfect Continuous | has/have been + V-ing | since/for + still ongoing |
| Simple Past | V2 | yesterday/ago/last, time emphasis, one-time historical fact |
| Past Perfect | had + V3 | first of two past actions; before/after; "it's time" (no—that's simple past); imaginary "if" clause takes simple past not this |
| Past Perfect Continuous | had been + V-ing | ongoing action up to a past point (not present) |
| Simple Future | will/shall + V1 | plain future action |
| Future Continuous | will be + V-ing | ongoing at a future point |
| Future Perfect | will have + V3 | completed by a future point ("by...") |
| Future Perfect Continuous | will have been + V-ing | ongoing continuously up to a future point |

## Chapter 11: Extra Depth & More Examples

### More Stative Verbs (dual-use, context decides)
| Verb | Stative sense (NO -ing) | Action sense (-ing OK) |
|---|---|---|
| **smell** | *This flower smells good* (state) | *He is smelling the flower* (deliberately sniffing) |
| **taste** | *The soup tastes good* (state) | *He is tasting the soup* (action of tasting) |
| **think** | *I think he is right* (opinion = stative) | *I am thinking about the plan* (mental process = action) |
| **weigh** | *The bag weighs 5 kg* (state) | *He is weighing the bag* (action of weighing on a scale) |
| **see** | *I see what you mean* (understand) | *I am seeing her tomorrow* (meeting — action) |
| **have** | possession → no -ing | eat/enjoy/attend/suffer/experience → -ing OK |

**Rule of thumb:** If the verb describes a **fixed quality/state of being**, no -ing. If the same verb describes someone **actively doing something**, -ing is fine.

### More on Conditional Trigger Words (besides "if")
All of these can make a sentence conditional, and the same rule applies — **no two future forms**, first part simple present/past, second part future/modal:
- **whenever** — *Whenever you come, please bring some sweets for me* (imperative, no will needed)
- **unless** (= if...not) — *You will not succeed unless you work hard* (note: "unless" already means negative, don't add "not" again)
- **until/till** — *Wait until I come back* (not "until I will come back")
- **provided (that)** (= basharte, conjunction meaning condition) — *You will succeed, provided you work hard* — NOTE: "provided" is also a plain verb (past tense of "provide": *I provided him with food and shelter*). As a **conjunction** meaning "on condition that", it takes the same rule as "if".
- **in case** — *Take an umbrella in case it rains* (not "in case it will rain")
- **after** — *I shall know the result after the letter reaches* (not "after the letter will reach"); if the letter has *already* arrived, use Present Perfect instead: *After the letter **has** reached...*
- **modal verbs used as conjunctions** — *Should he come, I will go* = *If he comes, I will go* (inversion using "should", no "if" needed)

### Present Perfect / Present Perfect Continuous — More Worked Examples
- *I have known him for two years* ✅ — CANNOT be *I know him for two years* ❌, even though "for + duration" sentences are sometimes Simple Present (habitual repeated action, e.g. *I work for two hours [every day]*). **Why the difference:** "knowing" someone is not a habitual, repeatable-every-day action — it's a continuous, unbroken state since a point in the past, so it must take Present Perfect, not Simple Present.
- *I have worked for two hours* (habitual, could mean routine 2 hrs daily and finished today) vs *I have been working for two hours* (still working right now, continuously) — both grammatically fine, sense differs.
- **"Off late"** = हाल फिलहाल (recently) — always takes Present Perfect: *Of late, there **has been** no news of his recovery.*
- **"Already"** — always Present Perfect: *The train **has** already left* (not "already left" without has, and not "was already left" — no passive needed here).

### Past Perfect — More Practice Sentences (with logic)
1. *The thief **had escaped** before I opened the door.* → escaping happened first (had + V3), opening the door second (Simple Past).
2. *The thief explained how he **had escaped** from jail.* → escaping happened first (had escaped), explaining came after (Simple Past — explained).
3. *On reaching the station, I found the train **had left**.* → train leaving happened first.
4. *As soon as he stepped onto the stage, it **collapsed**.* → SIMULTANEOUS actions → both Simple Past, NOT "had stepped" (no first/second sequence here).
5. Doctor–patient example (sequence depends on context, don't assume blindly):
   - *The patient **died** after the doctor **came**.* → doctor came first, patient died after (reverse of the common assumption!)
   - *The patient **had died** before the doctor **came**.* → patient died first (had died), doctor came later.
6. Campus/bus example: *On reaching the campus, he was disappointed as the bus **had** already **left**.*
7. Airport example: *We reached his house on time, but he **had** already **left** for the airport.*

### "Would have" vs "Would" — More Nuance
- *If Mohan had been in our team, we **would have won** the match.* → Correct, because "won" refers to a match that is now **over** — the action (match) is complete, so BOTH clauses must be Perfect:
  - *If Mohan had been (had + V3) in our team, we would have won (would have + V3) the match.* ✅
  - NOT *If Mohan was in our team, we would win the match* ❌ — because winning refers to a completed/past opportunity, not a general/future one.
- Compare with a **general/repeatable** situation (not a specific completed event): *If he worked hard, he would succeed* (Simple Past + would — general truth, not one completed occasion) — both Simple Past is fine here since nothing is marked "completed."

### Present Perfect vs Present Perfect Continuous — "Since/For" with a Fixed Number
- *The player has been scoring century after century since 2016* ✅ (open-ended, ongoing achievement)
- *The player has scored 10 centuries since 2016* ✅ (fixed, completed count — must use Perfect, NOT Perfect Continuous)
- ❌ *The player has been scoring 10 centuries since 2016* — wrong, because a **fixed number** signals the action is complete, not still ongoing.

### Additional Note on "Seen/Watched/Singing" type words
- *I am watching the movie* ✅ (watch = deliberate ongoing action, has -ing)
- *I am seeing the movie* ❌ (see ≠ watch when it means "perceive with eyes" in this active continuous sense — though "seeing" is fine when it means "meeting" someone or used as a gerund subject: *Seeing is believing.*)
- *I have seen the movie* ✅ (perfect — completed viewing)
- When **-ing functions as a noun** (subject/object of the sentence — a gerund) rather than as the main verb, it's grammatically fine regardless of the verb's usual restrictions: *Singing (as a gerund) is his passion.*

### Present Perfect: "Today / This week" priority rule
- If BOTH Simple Past and Present Perfect are grammatically valid options for "today/this week" type sentences, prefer:
  - **Present Perfect** when the period (today, this week) has **not yet ended**
  - **Simple Past** when logically the period would already be treated as over in context
- Example: *I have seen her today* (preferred over *I saw her today*, since "today" isn't over yet) — but if both appear as options, choose the Present Perfect one.

---

## Most Common Exam Traps (Summary)
1. Time word present → check if it's stressing **time** (Simple Past) or **linking two actions** (Past Perfect)
2. "Yesterday" ≠ always Simple Past
3. Stative verbs (have-possession, know, belong, appear-seem, hear-listen, like/love, understand, believe) never take -ing UNLESS meaning shifts to an action sense
4. Conditional sentence: never two "will"/two "would have" — if-clause takes simple form, main clause takes future/modal form
5. Imaginary/hypothetical "if" and "as if/as though"/"I wish" → always Simple Past (V2), and "were" for all subjects including singular
6. Two "had" in a sentence CAN be correct (auxiliary + main verb); two "have/has" CANNOT
7. "It's time / It's high time / It's about time" → always followed by Simple Past
8. Fixed/completed number (e.g., "10 centuries") → Perfect (not Perfect Continuous); ongoing/unspecified count → either works
9. "By + future time" → Future Perfect, not Simple Future

## B. Present Continuous

**Examples:**
1. She **is writing** (write) a letter right now.
2. They **are playing** (play) football at the moment.

**Exercises:**
