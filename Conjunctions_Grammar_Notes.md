# Conjunctions — Exam Notes
*Source: Conjunction lecture (English Grammar for SSC/Competitive Exams)*

---

## 1. What is a Conjunction?
"Conjunction" comes from "junction" — joining. A conjunction is a **joining word**. It is the most "loyal" part of speech: it joins like with like — noun with noun, verb with verb, adjective with adjective, pronoun with pronoun.

**Conjunction joins 4 things:** Words, Phrases, Clauses, Sentences.

- *He is honest **and** hardworking.* (word-word)
- *I will meet you at home **and** at office.* (phrase-phrase)
- *He said **that** he would come.* (clause-clause)
- *He was ill. **Since** he was ill, he could not come.* (sentence-sentence, joined into one)

## 2. Phrase vs Clause (Foundation Concept)
**Phrase** = a group of 2+ words with **no Subject-Verb combination** → gives **no meaningful sense** on its own.
- *at home*, *at office*, *at an arm's length* (idiom/phrase)

**Clause** = a group of words **with a Subject-Verb combination**. A clause *may or may not* give meaningful sense depending on its type.

**Examples:**
1. *at home* / *at office* — phrases, no subject-verb, no meaning alone
2. *if he comes* — has subject (he) + verb (comes) = a clause
3. *he said* — has subject (he) + verb (said) = a clause

## 3. Independent vs Dependent Clause
- **Independent Clause**: gives complete/meaningful sense on its own; doesn't need support.
- **Dependent Clause**: has subject-verb but does **NOT** give complete sense alone; needs another clause's support.

A sentence can have: two independent clauses, OR one independent + one dependent. **Never two dependent clauses alone** (that would give no meaning at all).

**Examples:**
1. *If he comes* — incomplete alone → **Dependent**. *I will go* — complete alone → **Independent**. (*If he comes, I will go.*)
2. *He said* — incomplete alone (said what?) → **Dependent**. *He would come* — complete alone → **Independent**. (*He said that he would come.*)
3. *He was ill* — complete → **Independent**. *He could not attend office* — complete → **Independent**. (Both independent, joined by "as": *As he was ill, he could not attend office.*)

Conjunction's job: joins clauses too (not just words/phrases).

---

## 4. Conjunction vs Preposition — The Core Distinction
Many word-pairs look like they mean the same thing (cause, contrast, purpose), but one is a **Conjunction** (joins a Clause: needs Subject+Verb after it) and the other is a **Preposition** (takes an Object: needs a Noun/Pronoun/Gerund after it, not a full clause).

**Rule of thumb:**
- If what follows has **Subject + Verb** → **Conjunction** needed.
- If what follows is just a **Noun/Pronoun/Gerund (no verb of its own)** → **Preposition** needed.

---

## 5. Although / Though (Conjunction) vs In spite of / Despite (Preposition)
Both show contradiction (a positive fact + a negative outcome, or vice versa), but:
- **Although / Though** = Conjunction → followed by Subject + Verb (a clause)
- **In spite of / Despite** = Preposition → followed by a Noun/Gerund (no separate verb)

**Examples:**
1. *Although he worked hard, he failed.* (he=subject, worked=verb → clause, so conjunction)
2. *In spite of working hard, he failed.* (working = gerund/noun, no subject-verb clause, so preposition)
3. *Though he was ill, he attended the party.* / *In spite of his illness, he attended the party.*

## 6. Contradiction (Adversative) Words — Full List
These show a positive-negative (or negative-positive) contrast between two parts of a sentence.

**Conjunctions:** But, Yet (when meaning "still/however"), Though/Although, While (when meaning "whereas"), Even though, Even if
**Prepositional phrases:** In spite of, Despite (Despite does NOT take "of"), Notwithstanding

**Examples:**
1. *He worked hard **but** failed.*
2. *He is rich **yet** he is a miser.*
3. *He deceived me, **still** I trusted him.* / *He deceived me, **nevertheless** I trusted him.*

**Important — "In spite of" vs "Despite":** "In spite" always takes "of" (in spite **of**), but **Despite never takes "of"** (despite, not despite of).

## 7. "But" — Two Meanings
- **But = "lekin" (but/however)** → acts as a **Conjunction**, joins two clauses, shows contradiction (positive-negative)
- **But = "except" (ke siwaay)** → acts as a **Preposition**, takes an object (noun), does NOT show contradiction

**Examples:**
1. *He worked hard **but** failed.* (but = "lekin", conjunction, joins 2 clauses)
2. *Nothing **but** books was available.* (but = "except", preposition; no clause, just "books" as object)
3. *Nobody **but** he can do this.* (but = "except", preposition)

**Test:** Check if there's a Subject+Verb on both sides — if yes, "but" is a conjunction (contradiction rule applies); if the word after "but" is only a noun/object with no clause, "but" is a preposition ("except") and contradiction rule doesn't apply. Sentence-level agreement (was/were) also follows the *subject before "but"*, not the object after it, when "but" = except.

## 8. "Yet" — Two Meanings
- **Yet = "phir bhi" (still/however)** → **Conjunction**, shows contradiction
- **Yet = "ab tak" (till now)** → **Adverb**, describes a verb (typically used with Present/Past Perfect tense), no contradiction, no clause-joining

**Examples:**
1. *He worked hard, **yet** he failed.* (yet = "phir bhi", conjunction, contradiction)
2. *He has not come **yet**.* (yet = "ab tak", adverb, describing "come", perfect tense)
3. *He started late, **yet** he reached on time.* (yet = "phir bhi", contradiction: negative+positive)

**Rule:** "Yet" in the sense of "ab tak" is normally used with **Perfect tense**, and does not create contradiction structure.

## 9. Full List of Contradiction (Adversative) Conjunctions/Prepositions
Meaning "phir bhi / lekin / ke bawajood":
- **But, Yet, Still, However, Nevertheless, Nonetheless, Although, Though, Even if, Even though** — Conjunctions
- **In spite of, Despite, Notwithstanding** — Prepositions

**Examples:**
1. *He is rich **but** he is a miser.*
2. *He deceived me; **however**, I forgave him.*
3. *He worked hard; **nevertheless**, he failed.*

---

## 10. "While" — Two Meanings
- **While = "jabki/whereas" (contrast)** → Conjunction (contrast between two people/things)
- **While = "ke dauraan" (during)** → Preposition, taken before a Gerund (noun form of verb), no separate subject-verb clause after it

**Examples:**
1. *He likes writing, **while** his brother likes reading.* (contrast between two people → conjunction)
2. *He got hurt **while** driving.* (while + gerund "driving" = preposition, "during")
3. *She was smart, **while** her brother was a fool.* (contrast, conjunction)

**Test:** If "while" is followed by Subject+Verb (a full clause) with a genuine contrast between two subjects → conjunction. If followed by only a gerund (no separate subject) → preposition ("during").

---

## 11. Reason-showing Words — Because/As/Since/For (Conjunctions) vs Because of/Due to/Owing to (Prepositions)
- **Because, As, Since, For** = Conjunctions → followed by Subject+Verb clause
- **Because of, Due to, Owing to** = Prepositions → followed by Noun (no separate clause)

**Examples:**
1. *He could not come **because** he was ill.* (clause after "because" → conjunction)
2. *The match was cancelled **because of** bad weather.* / *...**due to** bad weather.* (noun "weather" after → preposition)
3. *As he was ill, he could not attend.* / *Since he was ill, he could not attend.*

**"Since" special note:** "Since" can be a conjunction (reason OR time) *and* a preposition (time only). If followed by a noun/time-point (e.g., "since Monday") → preposition. If followed by Subject+Verb → conjunction.
- *I have been ill **since** Monday.* (preposition, time point)
- *I have not met him **since** he left.* (conjunction, "he left" = clause, meaning "since then")
- *Since he was ill, he could not come.* (conjunction, reason)

**"For" vs "Since":** "Since" is used for a **point of time**; "for" is used for a **duration**.
- *I have been driving **for** 10 hours.* (duration → for)
- *I have been ill **since** Monday.* (point of time → since)

## 12. "The reason" — With "That", NOT "Because"
When a sentence already has "**The reason**..." explicitly stating the cause, do NOT add "because" (that would be redundant — "because" is itself a conjunction meaning reason). Use "**that**" instead.
- If instead of "because", "due to"/"owing to" (prepositions) are wrongly used after "the reason", they should simply be **removed** (no word needed) — because you can't replace a wrong preposition with a conjunction.

**Examples:**
1. *The reason **why** he failed was **that** he was careless* (not "because he was careless")
2. *The reason **for** his failure was **that** he was too young.* (not "because...")
3. *The reason for his rejection was **that** he was too young.* (not "due to"/"because")

---

## 13. Purpose-showing Words — In order to / So that / In order that / So (Result)
- **In order to + V1** = Preposition (needs plain infinitive, no separate clause)
- **In order that / So that** = Conjunction (needs a clause: Subject + Verb)
- **So** (alone) = Conjunction showing **Result** ("therefore/isliye"), not purpose

**Examples:**
1. *Students work hard **in order to** get good marks.* (V1 "get" directly, no clause → preposition-type usage)
2. *We eat **so that** we may live.* / *...**in order that** we may live.* (clause "we may live" after → conjunction, purpose)
3. *He worked hard, **so** he succeeded.* (result, not purpose — "so" alone means "therefore")

**Test for "in order to" vs "so that":** If only a Subject exists with **no separate verb** of its own after it (needs V1 form directly) → preposition-type ("in order to"). If a full clause (new Subject + Verb, e.g., "he would...") follows → conjunction ("so that").
- *He went to the doctor **in order to** have a medical checkup.* (no separate subject-verb, just "have" = V1)
- *He went to the doctor **so that** he would have a medical checkup.* (separate clause "he would have")

**"So that" vs split "So...that":** When "so" and "that" are used together immediately, they show **Purpose**. When "so" and "that" are split apart by an adjective in between, they show **Result**.
- *He encouraged me **so that** I might not lose heart.* (purpose, "so that" together)
- *He was **so** happy **that** he forgot to take his papers.* (result, "so...happy...that" split by adjective)

## 14. Two Conjunctions Joining the Same Idea — Not Allowed
If two conjunctions in one sentence show the exact same relationship (e.g., both showing condition, or one conjunction + one adverb both doing the same job), **only one should be used** — remove the redundant one.

**Examples:**
1. ❌ *Supposing if he is late, I will do the work.* → ✅ *Supposing he is late, I will do the work.* (both "supposing" and "if" show condition — keep only one)
2. ✅ *He was ill, **and so** I took him to the doctor.* (correct — "and" joins clauses, "so" shows result; two **different** jobs, so both can stay)
3. ❌ *He was **so** happy **that** he forgot his papers* is correct as one relation; but splitting an already-joined "so that" (purpose) with an extra word incorrectly duplicates meaning — avoid redundant doubling of the same logical connector.

---

## 15. Condition-showing Conjunctions (Full List)
**Unless, If, Lest, Otherwise, Else, Provided, In case, Supposing, Only if** — all denote condition ("agar/jab tak/basharte/anyathaa").

**Examples:**
1. *I will take an umbrella **in case** it rains.*
2. *You will succeed **only if** you work hard.*
3. *He will succeed **provided (that)** he works hard.*

### "Provided" — Conjunction vs Verb
"Provided" can be a **Conjunction** (meaning "basharte/provided that" — condition) or a plain **Verb** (past tense of "provide", meaning "supplied/arranged").
- *He **was provided** with the basic needs.* (verb — "provided" = supplied)
- *He will succeed, **provided** he works hard.* (conjunction — condition)

---

## 16. Unless vs Until — Core Differences
- **Unless** = denotes **Condition** ("jab tak nahi" in a conditional sense) — **ALWAYS acts as a Conjunction only**, never a preposition.
- **Until** = denotes **Time** ("jab tak") — can act as **both a Conjunction AND a Preposition**.

**Examples:**
1. *I will wait **until** Monday.* (Until + noun "Monday" → Preposition, time point)
2. *I will wait **until** he comes.* (Until + clause "he comes" → Conjunction)
3. *Unless you work hard, you will not succeed.* (Unless = condition, always Conjunction)

**Practical tip:** In a sentence, if it's telling you a **time** → use Until; if it's telling you a **condition** → use Unless. Both can appear as clause-joiners (conjunctions), so choose based on **meaning**, not just grammar form.

## 17. Unless / Until — Three Special Error Rules
1. **Unless and Until are themselves negative words** → **NEVER use "not"** with the clause that directly follows Unless/Until.
2. Sentences with Unless/Until generally take **Simple Present tense** (not Future "will") in the clause that follows them.
3. If the sentence structure is reversed (Unless/Until clause comes *after* the main clause instead of before), the "not"-removal rule applies to whichever clause **directly follows** Unless/Until — not always to a fixed "first" or "second" part.

**Examples:**
1. ❌ *Unless you **don't** work, you will not succeed.* → ✅ *Unless you work, you will not succeed.* (no "not" right after "unless")
2. ❌ *Unless you **will** work, you will not succeed.* → ✅ *Unless you work, you will not succeed.* (no "will" right after "unless"/"until")
3. ✅ *I cannot afford to go on the trip **unless** I win the lottery.* (simple present "win", no "not", no "will")

**Logic:** *If + not = Unless.* So Unless already carries "not" inside its meaning — adding another "not" is a double-negative and changes the meaning.

## 18. If vs Unless — Structural Difference
- With **If**: "not" can appear on **either side or both sides** of the sentence (both positive, or both negative — the two halves move together).
- With **Unless**: since Unless is inherently negative, **only one part of the sentence carries "not"** (the other part stays positive) — because Unless itself supplies the negative meaning.

**Examples:**
1. *If he doesn't work, he will not succeed.* (both negative) / *If he works, he will succeed.* (both positive) — both correct with "If"
2. *Unless he works, he will not succeed.* (only ONE "not" — in the main clause; the "unless" clause itself stays positive: "he works", not "he doesn't work")
3. ❌ *Unless he doesn't work, he will not succeed.* → wrong (double negative) → ✅ *Unless he works, he will not succeed.*

## 19. "Lest" — Special Rules
"Lest" means "kaheen aisa na ho ke / anyathaa" (in case/lest). Three things to remember:
1. **Lest always takes "should"** as its modal verb (if any modal is used).
2. **Lest is negative in itself** → never use "not" with it.
3. Sentences with "Lest" are generally in **Simple Present tense**.
4. Sometimes "Lest" appears **without any modal at all** — that's also acceptable (not compulsory to always use "should").

**Examples:**
1. ❌ *Work hard, lest you **might** fail.* → ✅ *Work hard, lest you **should** fail.*
2. ❌ *Work hard, lest you should **not** fail.* → ✅ *Work hard, lest you should fail.*
3. ✅ *Walk properly, lest you **should** fall.* / ✅ *Walk properly, lest you **fall**.* (both correct — modal optional, but if used, must be "should")

**Lest = Otherwise = Else** (all three mean the same: "anyathaa/warna").
- *Work hard, **lest** you should fail.* = *Work hard, **otherwise** you will fail.* = *Work hard, **else** you will fail.*

---

## 20. Although/Though + Yet — Combination Rule
"Although/Though" and "yet" together in one sentence show contradiction — this is generally acceptable in **translation-based** (Hindi to English) sentences, but is often considered incorrect in strict SSC-level error-spotting because "although/though" already provides the contradiction meaning. However, this is **not a strict rule** — sometimes "yet" can be dropped without it being an error, and sometimes a **comma** replaces "yet" and is still counted correct.

**Examples:**
1. *Although it was difficult, (yet) I did it at any cost.* — "yet" here is often optional/dropped
2. *Although he worked hard, he failed.* (comma instead of yet — also correct)
3. *Though he tried his best, he failed.* — no "yet" needed

**Note:** No strict rule that "yet" is compulsory or always wrong — judge from context; comma alone is acceptable.

## 21. As...As vs So...As — Comparison Rules
"As...as" and "so...as" are used for **Comparison** — comparing either the **qualities (adjectives)** of two things, or an **action performed (adverb)** by two people.

**Key Rules:**
1. **As...as** → used in BOTH Positive and Negative sentences.
2. **So...as** → used ONLY in Negative sentences.
3. Whatever adjective/adverb comes **between** "as...as" or "so...as" must ALWAYS be in the **Positive Degree** — NEVER Comparative Degree. (Because "as...as"/"so...as" is *already* doing the comparison job; adding a comparative degree word like "better", "wiser" duplicates the comparison.)
4. Comparison needs **TWO "as"** (or "so...as") — a single "as" cannot create a comparison.

**Examples:**
1. *He is **as** tall **as** his brother.* (positive sentence, positive degree "tall")
2. *He is not **as/so** tall **as** his brother.* (negative sentence — both "as...as" and "so...as" work here)
3. ❌ *He is **as better as** his brother.* → ✅ *He is **as good as** his brother.* (positive degree "good", not comparative "better")

### As...As with Adjective or Adverb both
The word between "as...as" can be an **Adjective** (describing a noun/pronoun) or an **Adverb** (describing a verb) — both stay in Positive Degree.
- *He is **as fast as** his brother (is).* (fast = adjective, describing "he")
- *He works **as fast as** his brother (does).* (fast = adverb here, describing "works")
- *He worked **as slowly as** his brother did.* (slowly = adverb)

**Examples:**
1. *He is **as fast as** his brother.* (adjective)
2. *He acted **as nobly as** his brother.* (adverb — note: "nobly", not "more nobly")
3. *She is **as respectful as** Gandhiji.* (positive sentence, positive degree "respectful")

### As...As can be Positive OR Negative; So...As only Negative
- *These job opportunities are not **as/so** good **as** they used to be.* (negative — both work)
- *He is not **as** tall **as** his brother* = *He is not **so** tall **as** his brother* (both correct)

## 22. Like (Preposition) vs As (Conjunction)
- **Like** = Preposition → followed by a Noun/Pronoun (Object). No separate clause after it.
- **As** = Conjunction → followed by a full Clause (Subject + Verb).

**Examples:**
1. *He sings **like** me.* ("me" = pronoun/object, no verb after → Like = preposition)
2. *He sings **as** I do.* ("I do" = clause with its own subject+verb → As = conjunction)
3. *I don't want blue pens, I want red ones, **like** yours.* (like + noun → preposition)

**Test:** If nothing follows except a noun/pronoun (no verb) → Like. If a full clause with its own subject-verb follows → As.

## 23. Unlike (Preposition)
"Unlike" is always a **Preposition** (opposite of "like" as preposition), meaning "not like/different from". Since it's a preposition, whatever follows it is the **Object** — but for subject-verb agreement in the *main clause*, always check the **main Subject** of the sentence, not the object of "unlike".

**Examples:**
1. *Ram, **unlike** his brothers, **is** smart.* (subject = Ram (singular) → verb "is", NOT "are" to match "brothers")
2. *He, **unlike** his classmates, works hard.*
3. *She, **unlike** her friends, prefers reading.*

---

## 24. Neither...Nor vs Either...Or
- **Neither** takes **Nor** (never "and")
- **Either** takes **Or** (never "and"/"else")

**Examples:**
1. *He is **neither** intelligent **nor** hardworking.*
2. *Either** tell me the whole story **or** tell me part of it.*
3. ❌ *Neither...and* / ❌ *Either...else* — both wrong pairings

## 25. Not Only...But Also
"Not only" pairs with "But also" (not "however"). Sometimes "but" and "also" get **split apart** in the sentence — that is also correct, not an error.

**Examples:**
1. *He is **not only** hardworking **but also** honest.*
2. *He believes **not only** in hard work **but also** in honesty.*
3. *He **not only** started his own business **but** his friends **were also** present.* (split "but"..."also" — still correct)

**Note on Parallelism:** "Not only...but also" is a paired (correlative) conjunction — whatever follows "not only" and "but also" must be the **same part of speech / same grammatical form** (noun-noun, verb-verb, gerund-gerund) and placed at the **correct position** immediately before the item being compared.
- *He **not only** opened a hospital **but also** an orphanage.* (both nouns, correctly placed)
- ❌ *He **not only** stopped coming **but also** going elsewhere.* → compare "coming" vs "going" (both gerunds) — "not only" must sit right before "coming", not before the verb "stopped".

---

## 26. Hardly / Scarcely — Adverbs of Frequency + Inversion
"Hardly" and "Scarcely" both mean "shayad hi kabhi" (rarely/hardly ever) but:
- **Hardly** = Adverb of **Frequency** (how often something happens — "hardly" = almost never happens)
- **Scarcely** = does NOT show frequency; used more generally

**Pairing rule:** Hardly/Scarcely + **When** (not "than"). Some books also allow "before".
**Inversion rule:** When a sentence **starts with** Hardly/Scarcely, the clause takes **Inversion** (Verb before Subject — auxiliary like "did/had" comes before the subject).

**Examples:**
1. *Hardly **had** he entered the room **when** it began to rain.* (inversion: had+subject reversed)
2. *Scarcely **had** he entered the room **when** it began to rain.*
3. ❌ *He hardly did the work* — if starting with Hardly: ✅ *Hardly **did** he do the work.* (inversion needed only when Hardly starts the sentence)

## 27. No Sooner...Than + Inversion
"No sooner" is an Adverb, and takes **"than"** (not "when"/"then"). Like Hardly/Scarcely, if the sentence **starts with** "No sooner", **Inversion** is required.

**Examples:**
1. *No sooner **had** he entered the room **than** it began to rain.*
2. ❌ *No sooner he had entered the room, then it began to rain.* → wrong pairing (then) and wrong order (no inversion)
3. *No sooner **did** she see the snake **than** she screamed.*

## 28. As Soon As — No Inversion, No "Than"
"As soon as" means the same as "No sooner...than" (jaise hi), but:
- Does **NOT** take "than" (because "as...as" is a Conjunction, not an Adverb)
- Does **NOT** take Inversion (no reversal of subject-verb) — because it isn't an adverb starting the sentence in the same way

**Examples:**
1. *As soon as he entered the room, it began to rain.* (no inversion, normal subject-verb order)
2. ❌ *As soon as he entered the room, **than** it began to rain.* → no "than" needed
3. *As soon as she saw the snake, she screamed.*

---

## 29. Both...And / Between...And
- **Both** always takes **And** (never "as well as", never "with"/"along with")
- **Between** always takes **And** (never "to", never "and" replaced with anything else)

**Examples:**
1. *He needs knowledge of **both** English **and** Chinese.*
2. *The choice **between** the glorious death **and** shameful life...*
3. ❌ *Both...as well as* / ❌ *Between...to* — both wrong

## 30. As Well As — Not With "Both"
"As well as" can be used alone in a sentence for comparison-like joining, but **never with "Both"** — if "Both" is present, only "and" can follow, never "as well as".
**Subject-verb rule with "as well as":** the verb agrees with the **first subject** (before "as well as"), unlike "Both...and" where the verb agrees with **both** subjects combined (plural).

**Examples:**
1. *Ram, **as well as** his friend, **is** present.* (verb follows first subject "Ram" → singular "is")
2. ❌ *Both Ram as well as his friend is present* → ✅ *Both Ram **and** his friend **are** present.* (Both needs "and", plural verb)
3. *Both personality **as well as** external looks matter greatly between brothers and sisters* → ❌ (should be "**and**" with Both, not "as well as")

---

## 31. Nothing Else But / Rather Than / No Other...Than
- **Rather** pairs with **Than** (never "and")
- **No other / Nothing else** pairs with **But** (not "than")

**Examples:**
1. *I have **no other** claim **but** my property.*
2. *I would **rather** have the bat **than** sit idle.*
3. *He wants **nothing else but** to help others.*

---

## 32. WH-Word + "That" — Never Together
When converting Direct Speech to Indirect Speech, we normally add "**that**" as the joining conjunction. But when a sentence already has a **WH-word** (what, when, where, which, why, how, etc.) acting as the question/interrogative word, that WH-word itself does the conjunction's job — so **do NOT add "that"** before/with a WH-word.

**Examples:**
1. ❌ *He said to me **that** what I was doing.* → ✅ *He asked me **what** I was doing.* (no "that" needed, "what" itself joins)
2. ❌ *Bharat asked him **that** which was the way to the post office.* → ✅ *Bharat asked him **which** was the way to the post office.*
3. ✅ *He said **that** he was busy.* (no WH-word here, so "that" is correctly used)

### Exception — "When" can mean "Jab" (conjunction) too, not just "Kab" (interrogative)
"When" has two meanings: **"kab" (interrogative, asking a question)** and **"jab" (conjunction, meaning "at the time that")**. Only when "when" is being used in its **interrogative ("kab")** sense should "that" be avoided. When "when" means **"jab"** (a plain time-reference, not a question), it does NOT function as a WH-interrogative — so "that" CAN correctly appear alongside it.

**Examples:**
1. ✅ *He said **that when** he was a child, he used to help his mother.* ("when" = "jab", not a question → "that" is correct here)
2. ❌ *He asked me **that when** I would come.* → ✅ *He asked me **when** I would come.* ("when" = "kab", a real question → no "that")
3. Both sentence types can look similar in Hindi translation, so check whether "when" is asking a genuine question (no "that") or just marking a time reference within a statement (that + when is fine).

## 33. Seldom — If/Ever vs If/Never
"Seldom" means "shayad hi kabhi" (rarely). It pairs correlatively with **Ever** or **Never**:
- **Seldom + If + Ever** = "shायad hi kabhi ya kabhi-kabhaar" — slightly less negative (allows for rare occurrence)
- **Seldom + Never** = highly/strongly negative (almost "never at all")

**Rule:** Don't simply swap "ever" to "never" (or vice versa) to "fix" a sentence — that changes the meaning entirely. Instead, choose based on what the **sentence's overall context/meaning** demands (strongly negative vs slightly-possible).

**Examples:**
1. *Seldom, **if ever**, does he help the poor.* (rare occasional help is possible)
2. *Seldom, **if never**, have I turned away a beggar without giving alms.* — if the sentence's context is strongly negative ("never done such a thing"), choose "never" over "ever" even though both structures exist
3. In fill-in-the-blank/error-spotting where both "seldom if ever" and "seldom if never" are options, pick based on whether the sentence logically implies a strongly negative ("always did X") or a mildly-possible sense.

## 34. Such (Adjective) vs So (Adverb)
- **Such** = Determiner/Adjective → always followed by a **Noun** (no degrees like comparative/superlative for determiners)
- **So** = Adverb → can be followed by an **Adjective** or another **Adverb** (describes them), never directly followed by a Noun

**Key rule: "Such" + a/an**
- "Such" can be immediately followed by **a/an + Noun** — because articles go with nouns, and "such" always relates to a noun.
- "So" is **never** immediately followed by **a/an** (since "so" doesn't take nouns).
- If "such/so" is followed *directly* by **a/an** → it must be **"such"**.
- If "such/so" is followed *directly* by an **Adjective/Adverb** (with **no article/noun right after it**) → it must be **"so"**.

**Examples:**
1. *Such boys disturb the class.* (such + plural noun "boys", no article, no singular)
2. *He is **such a** naughty boy that nobody likes him.* (such + a + adjective + noun — "a" comes right after "such")
3. *She is **so** brilliant that everyone admires her.* (so + adjective, no noun/article after it)

### Such/So + That — Result Clause
Both "such...that" and "so...that" can show **Result**, but:
- **Such** is used when a **Noun** is involved (such + noun...that)
- **So** is used when an **Adjective/Adverb** is involved (so + adjective/adverb...that)

**Examples:**
1. *It was **such a** cold afternoon **that** we stopped playing.* (noun "afternoon" present)
2. *The problem was **so** complicated **that** it could not be solved.* (adjective "complicated", no noun)
3. *He was **so** clever **that** everyone admired him.* (adjective, no noun)

**Tricky case — Article placed AFTER the adjective:**
If the article (a/an) appears **right after "such/so"** → use "such". But if the article appears **later, after an adjective has already come in between** → use "so" (because "so" is now modifying that adjective, and the noun/article comes afterward).
- *He is **such a** naughty boy that nobody likes him.* (a right after "such")
- *He is **so** naughty **a** boy that nobody likes him.* (adjective "naughty" comes first, then "a boy" — here "so" is used, describing "naughty")

**Examples:**
1. *He is **such a** naughty boy that nobody likes him.*
2. *He is **so** naughty **a** boy that nobody likes him.* (equally correct, different word order)
3. *She is **so** brilliant **a** girl that everyone admires her.*

### Nouns Formed from Adjectives via "-ly" — Watch Out
Some words look like adjectives but are actually **Nouns** (e.g., "coward" = noun for the trait, "cowardly" = adjective). If "-ly" is added to an **adjective**, it usually becomes an **Adverb** (e.g., quick→quickly). But if "-ly" is added to a **Noun**, it becomes an **Adjective** instead (e.g., coward→cowardly).
- *Idiot* (noun) vs *Idiotic* (adjective)
- *Fool* (noun) vs *Foolish* (adjective)
- *Coward* (noun) vs *Cowardly* (adjective) — adverb form: "in a cowardly manner"

**Examples:**
1. *He is **such a** coward that everybody dislikes him.* (coward = noun, so "such")
2. ❌ *He is **so** coward that everybody dislikes him.* → wrong, because "coward" is a noun, not adjective
3. *He is **an** idiot* (not "an idiot person" — "person" is redundant since "idiot" is already a noun); *He is **idiotic**.* (adjective form used differently)

## 35. Inversion — Basics
**Inversion** = when the **Verb comes before the Subject** (reversed from the normal Subject-then-Verb order). The word comes from "inverse" (उल्टा).

**Normal order:** Subject + Verb (*He is going to school.*)
**Inversion:** Verb + Subject (*Is he going to school?*) — typically seen in **Interrogative (question) sentences**.

But Inversion also happens in certain **non-question statements** when the sentence **starts with specific Adverbs**: Hardly, Scarcely, No sooner, No sooner...than.

**Rule:** Inversion with these adverbs applies **ONLY when the adverb starts the sentence**. If the same adverb appears in the **middle** of the sentence, NO inversion is needed.

**Examples:**
1. *Is he going to school?* (interrogative inversion)
2. *Hardly **did** she help me.* (Hardly starts sentence → inversion) vs *She hardly helps me.* (Hardly in middle → no inversion)
3. *No sooner **had** he entered than it began to rain.* (No sooner starts sentence → inversion)

## Quick Revision Table

| Conjunction | Preposition (equivalent) |
|---|---|
| Although / Though | In spite of / Despite (no "of") |
| Because / As / Since / For | Because of / Due to / Owing to |
| So that / In order that | In order to (+ V1) |
| As (comparison, +clause) | Like (+ noun/pronoun) |
| While (contrast, +clause) | While (during, +gerund) |
| Unless (always conjunction) | — |
| Until (time) | Until (time, as preposition too) |
| — | Unlike (opposite of like) |
| Rather...than | — |
| Lest / Otherwise / Else | — (all = "anyathaa") |

## High-Yield Exam Traps to Remember
1. Contradiction words: check if Subject+Verb clause follows (conjunction) or just a noun (preposition).
2. Unless/Until: never add "not" right after them; simple present tense; Unless=condition (always conjunction), Until=time (conjunction or preposition).
3. Lest: always "should" if any modal; never "not"; simple present.
4. As...as/So...as: word in between must be Positive Degree, never Comparative.
5. Such = noun follows; So = adjective/adverb follows (no noun/article directly after "so").
6. No WH-word + "that" together — except when "when" means "jab" (time), not "kab" (question).
7. Hardly/Scarcely+When and No sooner+Than — both need inversion **only if they start the sentence**.
8. As soon as — no inversion, no "than".
9. Both...and / Between...and — always "and", never "as well as"/"with"/"to".
10. Not only...but also — check correct parallel position and matching part of speech.
